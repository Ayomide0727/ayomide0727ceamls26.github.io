what_i_learned: |
  Today, I continued exploring ways to improve skin tone representation in our
  skin lesion dataset. Since many publicly available dermatology datasets do
  not include skin tone labels, I focused my research on the Individual
  Typology Angle (ITA), a method researchers use to estimate skin tone from
  images.

  To better understand both the theory and implementation of ITA, I used
  several AI tools throughout the day. I used ChatGPT, Claude, NotebookLM, and
  Perplexity AI to research how ITA works, understand the methodology proposed
  in the original paper, and compare different implementation approaches.
  Claude was especially helpful for helping me build the code pipeline and
  reasoning through each stage of the implementation while I verified that each
  step was working correctly.

  While reading the ITA paper and exploring the authors' other publications, I
  discovered another interesting approach called Skin Tone Analysis for
  Representation in Educational Materials (STAR-ED). Unlike ITA, STAR-ED uses
  machine learning to estimate skin tone. Discovering another approach made me
  realize there are multiple methods researchers are exploring to address skin
  tone representation, and I would like to learn more about how these methods
  compare.

  I also began implementing the ITA pipeline so that we can eventually create a
  Fitzpatrick skin tone label for images that currently do not have one. This
  could potentially allow us to evaluate our model's fairness across different
  skin tones more effectively.

  In addition to my research, I started an online machine learning course on
  classification algorithms that my mentor recommended. Each member of our
  research group is completing a different module, and once everyone finishes,
  we will teach one another what we learned so the entire team benefits from
  the course.

blockers: |
  My biggest challenge today was getting the ITA pipeline to produce accurate
  skin tone estimates. The results I am currently obtaining are noticeably
  incorrect, so I spent a significant amount of time debugging and trying to
  identify possible causes.

  One issue I suspect is the nature of our merged dataset. Since our images come
  from multiple sources, some are dermoscopic images while others are standard
  clinical photographs taken under different lighting conditions and camera
  settings. This variation may affect the ITA calculation.

  Another possible issue is that many images do not contain enough visible
  healthy skin surrounding the lesion. Since ITA requires healthy skin regions
  for segmentation and analysis, the lack of exposed skin may be producing
  inaccurate estimates.

reflection: |
  Today reminded me that research rarely follows a straight path. What started
  as a search for additional datasets turned into a much deeper investigation
  into skin tone estimation methods and fairness in medical AI.

  I also realized that AI tools can be powerful research assistants when used
  thoughtfully. Rather than relying on a single tool, I used ChatGPT,
  NotebookLM, Perplexity AI, and Claude for different purposes—from
  understanding research papers and comparing methodologies to helping build
  and debug my implementation. Combining these tools with reading the original
  research papers helped me develop a much stronger understanding of ITA than I
  would have gained from using only one resource.

  Although my pipeline is not yet producing the correct results, I now have a
  better understanding of the challenges involved in estimating skin tone from
  medical images. Tomorrow, I plan to continue debugging the implementation and
  investigate whether dataset characteristics, image type, or segmentation are
  responsible for the inaccurate ITA values.
