what_i_learned: |
  Today, I completed the machine learning classification course that my mentor
  recommended. The course strengthened my understanding of different
  classification algorithms and gave me a better foundation for evaluating
  machine learning models. Since each member of our team completed a different
  module, I am looking forward to sharing what I learned and learning from my
  teammates' modules as well.

  I also continued researching the Individual Typology Angle (ITA) for skin tone
  estimation. The more I researched, the more I discovered that each paper led
  to additional papers, implementations, and ideas. While discussing my
  progress with my graduate mentor, I came across a recent research paper and
  an accompanying GitHub repository that implements skin tone estimation. My
  mentor encouraged me to explore the existing implementation first before
  trying a completely different approach.

  As I worked through the code, I realized how much there still is for me to
  learn. The implementation consists of multiple Python files that depend on
  one another, making it difficult to understand how the entire pipeline fits
  together. Instead of becoming discouraged, I started breaking the problem
  into smaller pieces so I could better understand each component.

  During my dataset search, I also discovered the PASSION dataset, which
  contains pigmented skin lesion images from Sub-Saharan Africa. Although it
  does not focus on skin cancer and therefore is not suitable for our current
  classification task, it reminded me how much dermatology data exists outside
  of the commonly used datasets.

blockers: |
  Today was one of the most challenging research days I have had so far. My
  biggest obstacle was understanding the existing ITA implementation. The code
  references several Python files that work together, making it difficult to
  determine where the pipeline begins and how each component interacts with the
  others.

  I also ran into Claude usage limits while using it to help me understand and
  debug parts of the implementation. Having to pause my work while waiting for
  the usage limit to reset interrupted my workflow and slowed my progress.

reflection: |
  Although today was challenging, I learned an important lesson about research:
  progress is not always measured by how much code you write. Sometimes the
  biggest achievement is developing a deeper understanding of a difficult
  problem.

  I found myself feeling overwhelmed at times because every answer seemed to
  lead to another research paper, another implementation, or another question.
  Rather than trying to understand everything at once, I decided to slow down,
  focus on one step at a time, and build my understanding gradually.

  My goal for tomorrow is to develop a clearer implementation plan before
  writing more code. I want to verify a skin tone estimation pipeline using
  datasets that already have Fitzpatrick or DDI skin tone labels. If the
  results are reliable, I can then apply the pipeline to unlabeled datasets to
  estimate skin tones and support fairness evaluation in our skin lesion
  classification project.
