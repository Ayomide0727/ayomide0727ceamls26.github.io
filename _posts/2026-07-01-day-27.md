what_i_learned: |
  Today, I focused on writing the methodology section of our research paper.
  Specifically, I worked on the data collection and dataset description section,
  where I documented the datasets used in our project and explained how they
  were prepared for model training. After completing my writing, I began searching for additional skin lesion
  datasets that could strengthen our project. One of the main requirements was
  finding datasets that include skin tone labels so that we can better evaluate
  the fairness of our model across different skin tones.

  During my search, I discovered a concept called the Individual Typology Angle
  (ITA), which is a method researchers use to estimate skin tone from images.
  Learning about ITA introduced me to a new area of research related to skin
  tone classification and fairness in medical AI. I also explored whether this
  approach could potentially be used to generate skin tone labels for datasets
  that do not already include them.

blockers: |
  The biggest challenge today was finding publicly available skin lesion
  datasets with reliable skin tone labels. While there are many dermatology
  datasets available, only a few include information about patient skin tone,
  making it difficult to evaluate fairness across diverse populations.
  Because of this limitation, I spent a significant amount of time researching
  alternative methods for estimating skin tone and comparing different datasets.

reflection: |
  Today showed me that finding the right dataset is just as important as
  building the model itself. A model can only be as fair and reliable as the
  data it is trained and evaluated on.
  Discovering the Individual Typology Angle (ITA) was one of the highlights of
  the day because it opened up a new direction for my research. I plan to
  continue exploring this concept to determine whether it can help us improve
  our dataset and better evaluate fairness across different skin tones.

  Tomorrow, I hope to continue searching for suitable datasets and investigate
  how ITA and other skin tone estimation methods can support the goals of the
  DermaBridge project.
